Die with Zero — framework
Die with (Almost) Zero — the philosophy in numbers Ages 46 → 75+ ▼

Die with Zero is not recklessness — it is precision. The goal is to convert every euro of capital into the maximum possible life experience before death. Hoarding wealth you never use is a form of waste. The optimal trajectory is a deliberate, planned descent to zero.

Memory dividend
Experiences compound in memory. A trip at 50 is enjoyed in the moment and relived for 25 years. A trip at 75 is enjoyed once. Spend on experiences early — the return on memory is highest when health and energy are still full.
Health × money × time
The three resources of a rich life. The optimal zone — high health, enough money, free time — is the window this plan is designed to maximize. You enter it at 47. Most people never enter it at all.
The €20k floor
Not a nest egg to be guarded — a safety buffer for the final years. Covers emergency care, final logistics, and dignity. Everything above this threshold is to be used. Intentionally. Joyfully.
Bucket timing
47–60: adventure bucket — travel, physical, novelty. 60–70: depth bucket — culture, relationships, slower richness. 70–75: comfort bucket — peace, beauty, presence. Each decade has its own spending logic.

Final act — on your own terms
The Final Exit — Switzerland Planned · Timing TBD ▼

The plan includes a conscious, dignified conclusion in Switzerland — not a medical emergency, not a decision made under distress, but a considered and prepared final act. This is the ultimate expression of autonomy: choosing the terms of one's own exit.

Organizations
The two principal Swiss organizations are Dignitas (Forch, Zurich canton) and Exit International. Dignitas is the primary option for non-Swiss residents. Website: dignitas.ch — membership and consultation process should begin well in advance, while full mental competence is unquestionable.
Process & timeline
Dignitas requires: annual membership (~CHF 80/yr), a formal written request with full medical documentation, evaluation by independent Swiss physicians, and a consistent demonstration of wish over time. From first contact to final approval: typically 6–18 months. This is not a crisis service — it requires deliberate planning and documented competence.
Cost
Total cost including travel, accommodation, physician fees, and the procedure: approximately CHF 10,000–15,000 (~€10k–€16k). Reserve this as a dedicated line item within or alongside the €20k terminal buffer.
Legal status
Assisted dying is legal in Switzerland for non-residents meeting the criteria. French law is evolving but Switzerland remains the most reliable option for full autonomy. No criminal liability for accompanying persons under Swiss law.
Philosophical framing
This is not a footnote. It is the capstone. A life lived with full autonomy — financial, temporal, relational — should end with the same principle. Knowing the exit exists removes the fear of slow decline and gives permission to spend fully, live fully, and choose the moment.

This is not a financial plan. The numbers exist to serve a life — not the other way around. What follows is the philosophy that makes the numbers meaningful.

I — The Premise
Most people die with the wrong things.

They die with regrets about what they didn't do, with unspent savings they were afraid to touch, and with decades of delayed living waiting for a retirement that arrived too late, too tired, or not at all.

The conventional model is broken. Work 40 years. Accumulate. Retire at 65. Enjoy what's left. But health declines in your 60s. Energy peaks in your 40s and 50s. The window of vibrant, fully-lived life is not at the end — it's now.

This plan is a rejection of that model. It is an act of deliberate insubordination against the social script.

II — The Formula
Time × Health × Money. Only one combination matters.

A rich life requires three things simultaneously: time, health, and enough money. Most people spend the first half of life sacrificing time for money. The second half trying to buy time back with money. And they never quite have all three at once.

The goal of this plan is to secure all three before age 50. Not vast wealth — enough. Not unlimited time — free time. Not perfect health — the health of a calisthenics-trained, science-based 47-year-old with 30 good years ahead.

The window opens in 2027. The window is finite. Use it.

"The goal is not to die rich. The goal is to die having lived."
III — On Work
Work chosen is not work. Work required is a cage.

The barista FIRE model preserves the option to work without the obligation. A logistics mission in a warehouse, CACES certification in hand, taken because you wanted it — is categorically different from 18 years of showing up because the mortgage required it.

Chosen work has dignity. Required work has a price. This plan eliminates the price forever and restores the dignity.

If you never want to work another day, you won't have to. If you want the structure and the social energy of occasional missions, they're there. The certification is not a backup career — it's a freedom certificate.

IV — The Memory Dividend
Experiences compound. Objects decay. Money unspent is life unlived.

Bill Perkins' core insight: a memory, unlike a material purchase, pays dividends for the rest of your life. A trip to Colombia at 50 is experienced in the moment — and then remembered, relived, discussed, dreamed about for 25 more years. That is a 25-year return on a single investment.

The optimal moment to make a memory is when you have the health to live it fully, the time to be present for it, and the youth to carry it longest. That moment is not 65. It is now.

Spend on experiences early. Spend on depth in your 60s. Spend on comfort and peace in your 70s. Each decade has its own optimal allocation — and saving it all for the last one is the worst possible strategy.

V — On Frugality
€2,000 a month is not deprivation. It is precision.

The simulation is already underway. Constraining spending now — before the PSE — is not punishment. It is calibration. It answers the question every FIRE plan must answer honestly: is this number real, or is it a fantasy?

If €2,000/month in France feels comfortable during the test, it will feel comfortable in retirement. If it feels tight, adjust the plan. The discomfort now is infinitely cheaper than the discomfort of running out of money at 62.

The goal is not frugality as an identity. It is frugality as a tool — used to buy freedom, then set down.

VI — On Colombia
A second country is not a backup. It is a superpower.

Having viable roots in two countries means that no single economic environment can trap you. If France becomes too expensive, too socially turbulent, too cold — in every sense — you can leave. The Colombian assets aren't an emergency fund. They are proof that your life is not hostage to any single place.

Knowing you could leave changes how you feel about staying. The choice to remain in France, each day, becomes a genuine choice — not inertia, not necessity. That shift in agency is quietly transformative.

"Autonomy is not what you can afford to do. It is what you can afford to walk away from."
VII — Die with Zero
The €20k floor and the deliberate descent.

The terminal portfolio is not zero because you failed. It is zero because you succeeded. You converted every accumulated euro into the maximum possible life. You did not hoard. You did not defer. You lived in the years that living was most possible.

The €20k floor is not a safety net for heirs. It is dignity for the final years and coverage for the final logistics. Everything above it — every euro — is permission to live more fully, right now.

The descent from ~€378k to €20k, spread across 29 years of living, is not decline. It is the shape of a life fully spent.

VIII — The Final Act
The right to end. The freedom to choose.

The inclusion of Switzerland in this plan is not morbid. It is the final, fullest expression of the same philosophy that runs through every other phase: the belief that a person has the right to govern their own life — including its end.

Knowing the exit exists changes the texture of the decades before it. It removes the terror of slow decline, of loss of control, of a drawn-out ending dictated by biology alone. With that fear removed, the years of health are no longer shadowed by what waits at the end.

This is not a plan to die early. It is a plan to live freely — all the way to a chosen close.

The plan ends, as it began, with autonomy.

The three buckets — a life in phases

Die with Zero proposes spending not as a flat annual budget but as a series of bucket deployments, each matched to what the body and mind can actually enjoy at that stage of life.

Ages 47 – 60 · Adventure
Physical travel. New places. Calisthenics across continents. Chess tournaments. Long stays. The body is strong. Use it.
Ages 60 – 70 · Depth
Culture, philosophy, slower richness. Deeper relationships. Longer stays in fewer places. The mind at full power.
Ages 70 – 75 · Peace
Comfort, beauty, presence. The €20k buffer. Small joys. Witnessing. The portfolio has done its work.
